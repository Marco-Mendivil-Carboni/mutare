\section{Introduction and objectives} \label{sec:introduction}

Adaptation in fluctuating environments is a central problem in evolutionary biology \autocite{kingscollegeRiskUncertaintyEvolutionary1982}. Natural populations rarely experience constant conditions: rather, they face environments that change \emph{unpredictably} over time, often on ecological or evolutionary timescales. In such contexts, if sensing the changes in the environment is infeasible or too costly, it is well established in the ecology and population genetics literature that bet-hedging (i.e. investing only a fraction of the population to each possible phenotype) offers a good alternative to adapt to this fluctuating environment \autocite{kussellPhenotypicDiversityPopulation2005}.

In these circumstances our current understanding of evolutionary adaptation suggests that populations tend to evolve toward the strategy which maximizes their mean growth rate \autocite{gillespieNaturalSelectionVariances1977} \footnote{More precisely, \autocite{gillespieNaturalSelectionVariances1977} argues that what gets maximized is the geometric mean of the offspring number $\mu'$ (where $N(t+\Delta t)=\mu'\cdot N(t)$) but this is equivalent to maximizing the arithmetic mean of the growth rate $\langle\mu\rangle$ as we have defined it in \autoref{eq:avg_growth_rate}.}
\begin{equation} \label{eq:avg_growth_rate}
    \langle\mu\rangle=\left\langle\frac{1}{N(t)}\frac{dN}{dt}(t)\right\rangle.
\end{equation}
However, this is not an exact result for finite populations, and when environmental fluctuations are strong and extinction risk is non-negligible like in limited-size populations we expect to see deviations of this principle. In particular, we want to investigate whether natural selection in this conditions may favor strategies that sacrifice some average growth rate in exchange for a lower extinction rate $r_\text{ext}$.

Thus, the purpose of this work is to investigate, through numerical simulations, how the adaptation of finite populations in uncertain environments gives rise to phenotypic diversity and understand to what extent the strategies that emerge correspond to those of maximal growth or to safer, more resilient alternatives.

To adress the questions outlined in the introduction we introduce the simple agent-based model illustrated in \autoref{fig:model_diagram}. The state of the system consists of only two components: the \emph{environment} and the \emph{population}.

The \emph{environment} is simply a discrete variable with two possible values \footnote{Of course this is easily generalizable to the case of $n_{\text{env}}$ different environments.}, $e\in\{1,2\}$, which evolves according to a continuous-time Markov chain with transition rates $\omega_t(e,e')$ between states $e$ and $e'$ \footnote{The possibility that this environment variable follows non-Markovian dynamics or takes continuous values is outside of the scope of this work.}.

The \emph{population} is made up of $N$ agents, each one of which will be characterized just by two traits: a \emph{phenotype} $\phi\in\{A,B\}$\footnote{Again this could easily be generalized to more than two phenotypes.} and a \emph{phenotypic strategy} $s(\phi)$, which represents a probability distribution over the possible phenotypes. The phenotype will determine the response of the agent to the environment while the phenotypic strategy will determine the phenotype of the offsprings of that agent, introducing a mechanism for phenotypic diversity to appear and be mantained \emph{across generations}.

As we pointed out in the introduction we expect to see deviations from the already known principles of evolution when the population size does not grow indefinitely. Therefore, we will limit the population size to its initial value, $N_\text{ini}$: whenever replication would cause the population to exceed this value, a randomly chosen agent is simultaneously removed. This mechanism mimics continuous-culture evolution experiments \autocite{collectiveEconomicPrinciplesCell}, and can be interpreted as imposing an effective ecological carrying capacity.

Population dynamics occur through stochastic birth and death processes that depend on both the current environment and the phenotype of the agent. The matrices $\omega_b(e,\phi)$ and $\omega_d(e,\phi)$ denote the birth and death rates of phenotype $\phi$ in environment $e$, respectively. The net growth rate in a given environment thus depends on the phenotypic composition of the population.

When an agent replicates, its offspring inherits the parent's phenotypic strategy $s(\phi)$, but with probability $p_{\text{mut}}$ a mutation occurs, leading to a new random strategy. This mechanism allows for evolutionary exploration of the space of possible strategies.

If the population goes extinct, it is reinitialized, allowing for long-term statistical characterization of the dynamics.

To solve the stochastic model we will use the Gillespie algorithm [ADD CITE].

The model intentionally strips the problem down to its minimal ingredients. We consider only two environmental states and two phenotypes, and we restrict strategies to environment-independent stochastic switching. This simplicity is deliberate: our goal is not to reproduce the full biological complexity of real systems, but rather to isolate the fundamental tension between growth maximization and extinction avoidance in a fluctuating environment.

The crucial ingredient distinguishing our framework from standard models of bet-hedging is the explicit cap on population size. By imposing a maximum population size $N_i$, we effectively introduce a carrying capacity. Under this constraint, extinction becomes inevitable over sufficiently long times, even for strategies with positive mean growth rate. As a result, extinction is not a rare pathological event but an integral part of the long-term dynamics. This allows us to study how extinction risk feeds back into evolutionary selection.

Throughout the paper we focus on understanding whether evolution favors strategies that maximize mean growth rate, minimize extinction probability, or balance both objectives. The simplicity of the model allows us to compute and compare these quantities directly and to identify the regimes in which they conflict.

We begin by analyzing the model in the absence of evolution, that is, for fixed phenotypic strategies. This allows us to disentangle ecological effects from evolutionary ones and to characterize the trade-off between mean growth and extinction risk for each strategy independently.

Figure~\ref{fig:main_observables} shows the mean growth rate $\langle\mu\rangle$ and the extinction rate $r_e$ measured in fixed-strategy simulations for the asymmetric parameter set. The growth-maximizing strategy is, in general, distinct from the extinction-minimizing strategy. In particular, strategies that yield the highest average growth rate often exhibit substantially larger extinction rates. Conversely, safer strategies reduce extinction risk at the cost of lower mean growth.

This conflict demonstrates that, in finite populations subject to environmental fluctuations, growth maximization and survival maximization are not equivalent criteria. The existence of this trade off is the central ecological ingredient that will later shape the evolutionary outcome.

\section{Outcome of the evolutionary dynamics} \label{sec:evol_outcome}

We now enable evolution by allowing strategies to mutate and compete. Starting either from a monomorphic population or from a random distribution of strategies, the system consistently converges toward a well-defined stationary distribution in strategy space.

A key observation is that the instantaneous dispersion of strategies remains small for sufficiently low mutation probabilities. At any given time, the population is therefore almost monomorphic, characterized by a single dominant strategy. Over long times, however, mutation-driven exploration causes the dominant strategy to drift through strategy space. In this sense, the evolutionary simulations can be viewed as a temporal concatenation of quasi-monomorphic fixed-strategy realizations.

The average evolved strategy typically lies between the growth maximizing and extinction minimizing strategies identified in the fixed strategy simulations. This shows that evolution does not optimize instantaneous growth alone, but rather selects strategies reflecting a compromise between growth and extinction avoidance.

The evolutionary outcome depends systematically on both the population size $N_i$ and the mutation probability $p_{\mathrm{mut}}$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:dependence_on_N_ini_and_p_mut}). As $N_i$ increases, the evolved average strategy approaches the growth-maximizing value obtained from fixed simulations. This behavior is consistent with the rapid decrease of extinction risk with increasing population size.

By contrast, smaller populations experience stronger extinction pressure and evolve toward safer strategies. The mutation probability primarily controls the exploration of strategy space: for very large values, the population behaves almost as a randomized ensemble, while for very small values the system spends long periods near quasi-fixed strategies. Importantly, for sufficiently large $N_i$, the dependence on $p_{\mathrm{mut}}$ becomes weak, indicating that the evolutionary attractor is robust in the large-population regime.

Time series data confirm that, in the low-mutation regime, the population is effectively characterized by a common strategy at each instant (Fig.~\ref{fig:evidence_of_common_strategy}). The apparent breadth of the long-term strategy distribution arises from averaging over time and across realizations, not from coexistence of widely different strategies at a single moment.

This quasi-monomorphic behavior justifies interpreting the evolutionary process as a stochastic trajectory in strategy space, driven by rare successful mutations and shaped by extinction events. The fact that extinction events typically occur while the population is near a single strategy further supports this coarse-grained description.

\section{Explanation of the evolutionary outcome and a heuristic approximation} \label{sec:explanation_and_heuristic_approx}

To understand the evolutionary outcome, we return to fixed-strategy simulations and examine how growth and extinction scale with population size. While the mean growth rate $\langle\mu\rangle$ depends only weakly on $N_i$, the extinction rate $r_{\mathrm{ext}}$ decreases approximately as a power law, $r_{\mathrm{ext}}\sim N_i^{-\alpha}$. This strong size dependence implies that extinction pressure is significant for small populations but rapidly weakens as $N_i$ increases.

This observation suggests that the evolutionary attractor emerges from the competition between exponential growth amplification and algebraically suppressed extinction risk. Motivated by this idea, we propose the heuristic stationary distribution
\[
    p(s)\propto\frac{\exp(N_i\langle\mu(s)\rangle)}{r_{\mathrm{ext}}(s)+p_{\mathrm{mut}}\langle\mu_b(s)\rangle},
\]
constructed solely from quantities measured in fixed-strategy simulations.

The numerator captures the amplification of early growth differences when multiple strategies compete after reinitialization, while the denominator approximates the rate at which a strategy disappears, either through population extinction or mutation-driven replacement. Despite its simplicity, this expression reproduces the qualitative location and sharpening of the evolved distribution as $N_i$ increases.

\section{Discussion and outlook} \label{sec:discussion}

Our results provide a minimal and explicit example of how evolutionary outcomes in finite populations cannot be inferred solely from average instantaneous growth. The long-term dominant strategy depends not only on mean behavior, but also on the magnitude and direction of fluctuations, particularly those leading to extinction. In fluctuating environments with bounded population size, extinction is unavoidable over long times and therefore acts as a genuine selective force.

As population size increases, extinction risk becomes negligible and classical growth maximization is recovered. For smaller populations, however, extinction risk reshapes the adaptive landscape and biases evolution toward safer, lower-growth strategies. This crossover illustrates how demographic constraints fundamentally alter evolutionary optimization.

The heuristic approximation proposed here clarifies the competing scaling behaviors underlying this transition, even though it does not provide an exact quantitative description. The remaining discrepancies indicate that evolutionary dynamics cannot, in general, be reduced entirely to fixed-strategy observables. Developing a more systematic coarse-grained theory of strategy dynamics, possibly linked to quasi-stationary distributions, remains an important direction for future work.

More broadly, this study highlights that the effective fitness governing evolution in realistic finite populations must incorporate extinction risk explicitly. Understanding how such survival-weighted fitness functions emerge from stochastic ecological dynamics is essential for building a predictive theory of adaptation in fluctuating environments.
