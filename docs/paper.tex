\begin{abstract}
    Natural populations rarely evolve under stable conditions and without limitations: environments fluctuate unpredictably and resources constrain population size. In this work, we introduce a minimal stochastic model to study how evolutionary adaptation unfolds under these conditions. The model consists of a population undergoing a birth--death process in a randomly switching environment, where individuals carry heritable phenotypic strategies that determine the distribution of phenotypes across generations.

    By first analyzing fixed strategies, we show that environmental variability can favor phenotypic diversity through bet-hedging, and that strategies maximizing mean growth rate generally differ from those minimizing extinction risk. When evolution is enabled, populations do not simply maximize growth, but instead converge to intermediate strategies that balance reproductive performance and survival. This trade-off becomes particularly relevant in finite populations, where extinction risk acts as an additional selective pressure.

    We further demonstrate that population size controls the relative importance of these effects: large populations recover the classical growth-maximizing behavior, while smaller populations evolve toward safer, more resilient strategies. A simple heuristic approximation, constructed from fixed-strategy observables, captures the main features of the evolutionary outcome and clarifies the competing roles of growth and extinction.

    Overall, our results provide a clear picture of how environmental fluctuations and demographic constraints jointly shape evolutionary adaptation, highlighting the central role of extinction risk in determining evolutionary outcomes in finite populations.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction and objectives} \label{sec:introduction}

Adaptation in fluctuating environments is a central problem in evolutionary biology \autocite{kingscollegeRiskUncertaintyEvolutionary1982}. Natural populations rarely experience constant conditions: rather, they face environments that change \emph{unpredictably} over time, often on ecological or evolutionary timescales. In such contexts, if sensing the changes in the environment is infeasible or too costly, it is well established in the ecology and population genetics literature that bet-hedging (i.e. investing only a fraction of the population to each possible phenotype) offers a good alternative to adapt to this fluctuating environment \autocite{kussellPhenotypicDiversityPopulation2005}.

In these circumstances our current understanding of evolutionary adaptation suggests that populations tend to evolve toward the strategy which maximizes their mean growth rate \autocite{gillespieNaturalSelectionVariances1977} \footnote{More precisely, \autocite{gillespieNaturalSelectionVariances1977} argues that what gets maximized is the geometric mean of the offspring number $\mu'$ (where $N(t+\Delta t)=\mu'\cdot N(t)$) but this is equivalent to maximizing the arithmetic mean of the growth rate $\langle\mu\rangle$ as we have defined it in \autoref{eq:avg_growth_rate}.}
\begin{equation} \label{eq:avg_growth_rate}
    \langle\mu\rangle=\left\langle\frac{1}{N(t)}\frac{dN}{dt}(t)\right\rangle.
\end{equation}
However, this is not an exact result for finite populations, and when environmental fluctuations are strong and extinction risk is non-negligible like in limited-size populations we expect to see deviations of this principle. In particular, we want to investigate whether natural selection in this conditions may favor strategies that sacrifice some average growth rate in exchange for a lower extinction rate $r_\text{ext}$.

Thus, the purpose of this work is to investigate, through numerical simulations, how the adaptation of finite populations in uncertain environments gives rise to phenotypic diversity and understand to what extent the strategies that emerge correspond to those of maximal growth or to safer, more resilient alternatives.

To adress the questions outlined in the introduction we introduce the simple agent-based model illustrated in \autoref{fig:model_diagram}. The state of the system consists of only two components: the \emph{environment} and the \emph{population}.

The \emph{environment} is simply a discrete variable with two possible values \footnote{Of course this is easily generalizable to the case of $n_\text{env}$ different environments.}, $e\in\{1,2\}$, which evolves according to a continuous-time Markov chain with transition rates $\omega_t(e,e')$ between states $e$ and $e'$ \footnote{The possibility that this environment variable follows non-Markovian dynamics or takes continuous values is outside of the scope of this work.}.

The \emph{population} is made up of $N$ agents, each one of which will be characterized just by two traits: a \emph{phenotype} $\phi\in\{A,B\}$ \footnote{Again this could easily be generalized to more than two phenotypes.} and a \emph{phenotypic strategy} $s(\phi)$, which represents a probability distribution over the possible phenotypes \footnote{Since we restrict ourselves to two phenotypes, the strategy is fully specified by a single parameter, $s(A)$, as $s(B)=1-s(A)$. Throughout this work, we will therefore specify only this first component.}. The phenotype will determine the response of the agent to the environment while the phenotypic strategy will determine the phenotype of the offsprings of that agent, introducing a mechanism for phenotypic diversity to appear and be mantained \emph{across generations}.

In particular the population will follow a stochastic birth-death process in which the birth and death rates ($\omega_b(e,\phi)$ and $\omega_d(e,\phi)$ respectively) depend explicitly on both the current environment and the phenotype of each agent. Therefore, the behaviour of the population as a whole in a given environment will depend on its phenotypic composition.

Additionally, when an agent replicates, its offspring will generally inherit the parent's phenotypic strategy $s(\phi)$, but with probability $p_\text{mut}$ a mutation will occur, leading to a new random strategy \footnote{Thus, for simplicity, mutations in this model are assumed to be abrupt, even though this may not be the most common situation in biological systems. In \autoref{sec:incremental_mutations}, we extend the model by introducing an additional parameter to allow for incremental mutations, and show that this modification leads to qualitatively similar results, with only minor quantitative differences.}. This mechanism enables the evolutionary exploration of the space of possible phenotypic strategies, which constitute the heritable traits subject to selection, and therefore play the role of the \emph{genotype}.

Finally, as we pointed out in the introduction we expect to see deviations from the already known principles of evolution when the population size does not grow indefinitely. Therefore, we will limit the population size to its initial value, $N_\text{ini}$: whenever replication would cause the population to exceed this value, a randomly chosen agent is simultaneously removed. This mechanism mimics continuous-culture evolution experiments \autocite{collectiveEconomicPrinciplesCell}, and can be interpreted as imposing an effective ecological carrying capacity.

Despite the minimalism of the model, it is definitely impossible to solve it analytically. Therefore, we will perform simulations using the Gillespie algorithm \autocite{toralStochasticNumericalMethods} \footnote{More details about the simulations and the algorithm can be found in \autoref{sec:gillespie_algorithm}}. Still, as we will see in the next sections, the model is simple enough that its behavior can be readily analyzed, and at the same time it contains all the necessary elements to provide insight into the questions that motivate this work.

\section{Behaviour of the model without evolution} \label{sec:no_evolution}

We begin by solving the model without evolution, i.e. with $p_\text{mut}=0$ and assuming that all agents initially share the same strategy, $s_\text{ini}$. This kind of simulations (which we will call \texttt{fixed}) allow us to study the properties of individual strategies in isolation and, in turn, better understand the evolutionary outcomes that follow.

To proceed, we must first choose a specific set of model parameters. We start with the set shown in \autoref{tab:assymetric_parameters} \footnote{A completely different set of parameters is considered in \autoref{sec:symmetric_parameters}, which leads to the same main conclusions despite some differences.}. These parameters represent a common biological scenario in which there is a “resistant” phenotype and a “persistent” one \autocite{huftonPhenotypicSwitchingPopulations2018}. More precisely the meaning of this parameters is the following: both environments are equally likely and there is a transition rate between them of $1.0$; environment $1$ represents a ``favorable'' environment in which phenotype $A$ (the ``resistant'' one) replicates with a rate of $1.0$ while phenotype $B$ (the ``persistent'' one) does it with a rate of $0.2$, and neither of them die; environment $2$ instead represents ``harsh'' conditions in which phenotype $A$ dies with a rate of $1.0$ and phenotype $B$ with a rate of $0.1$, and neither of them are able to duplicate.

The strategy that maximizes mean growth (marked with a dashed line) differs from the one that minimizes extinction (marked with a dotted line), revealing a clear trade-off between these two possible objectives.} \label{fig:main_observables}

\autoref{fig:main_observables} shows the mean growth rate $\langle\mu\rangle$ and the extinction rate $r_e$ measured in fixed-strategy simulations for this parameter set. The first important observation to make is that the maximum of $\langle\mu\rangle$ occurs neither at $s(A)=0$ nor at $s(A)=1$, indicating that in cases such as this one phenotypic diversity (that is, maintaining a mixture of both phenotypes) is the ``optimal'' strategy, a result already reported multiple times \autocite{xueBetHedgingDemographic2017}. As we will see, this is precisely the strategy reached by evolution at large $N_\text{ini}$. This behavior does not arise in all cases, but only when environmental fluctuations make each phenotype individually less favorable in the long term than their combination.

Secondly, we observe that the growth-maximizing strategy differs from the extinction-minimizing one \footnote{Again, this occurs for other parameter sets, as discussed in \autoref{sec:symmetric_parameters}.}. In particular, $\langle\mu\rangle$ is maximized at $s(A)\sim0.8$, while $r_\text{ext}$ is minimized at $s(A)\sim0.0$. As we will show later, this trade-off (and more precisely the different scaling of these two quantities with the population limit) plays a key role in shaping the evolutionary outcome.

We do not explore in detail how these results depend on the specific choice of parameters, as this has been extensively studied in previous work. Instead, we focus in the next section on the outcome of the evolutionary dynamics and on which strategies are ultimately selected under these conditions.

\section{Outcome of the evolutionary dynamics} \label{sec:evol_outcome}

The dashed line indicates the strategy that maximizes mean growth, while the dotted line marks the one that minimizes extinction. The evolved strategy lies between these two, reflecting a compromise between growth and survival.} \label{fig:evol_results}
    \includegraphics{../sims/asymmetric/plots/strat_phe_0_i/dist_avg_strat_phe_0.pdf}
    \includegraphics{../sims/asymmetric/plots/strat_phe_0_i/avg_avg_strat_phe_0.pdf}
\end{figure}

We now enable evolution by allowing strategies to mutate and compete. We consider two types of simulations: in \texttt{evol} simulations, the population is initially composed of agents with the same strategy $s(A)_\text{ini}$, while in \texttt{evol(r)} simulations, the initial strategy of each agent is completely random.

To characterize the outcome of the evolutionary process, at each time we compute the population-averaged strategy $\overline{s}(A)$. In \autoref{fig:evol_results} we show the distribution of $\overline{s}(A)$ over time (top panel) and its time average (bottom panel). In both types of simulations, the system consistently converges toward a well-defined stationary distribution of $\overline{s}(A)$, with only a weak dependence on the initial condition.

A key observation is that the instantaneous dispersion of strategies $\sigma_s$ (defined as the spread of strategies present in the population at a given time) remains small. This dispersion is represented in the bottom panel of \autoref{fig:evol_results} as an error band, which is barely visible. At any given time, the population is therefore effectively characterized by a single dominant strategy. Over longer times, however, mutation-driven exploration causes this dominant strategy to drift through the space of possible strategies.

More importantly, we observe that the average evolved strategy lies between the growth-maximizing and extinction-minimizing strategies identified in the fixed-strategy simulations. As we will see in later sections, this behavior is not specific to this parameter set. This shows that evolution in finite populations does not optimize instantaneous growth alone, but instead selects strategies that represent a compromise between growth and extinction avoidance.

Next, in \autoref{fig:dependence_on_N_ini_and_p_mut} we explore how the evolutionary outcome depends on population size $N_i$ and the mutation probability $p_\text{mut}$.

The evolutionary outcome depends systematically on both the population size $N_i$ and the mutation probability $p_\text{mut}$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:dependence_on_N_ini_and_p_mut}). As $N_i$ increases, the evolved average strategy approaches the growth-maximizing value obtained from fixed simulations. This behavior is consistent with the rapid decrease of extinction risk with increasing population size.

By contrast, smaller populations experience stronger extinction pressure and evolve toward safer strategies. The mutation probability primarily controls the exploration of strategy space: for very large values, the population behaves almost as a randomized ensemble, while for very small values the system spends long periods near quasi-fixed strategies. Importantly, for sufficiently large $N_i$, the dependence on $p_\text{mut}$ becomes weak, indicating that the evolutionary attractor is robust in the large-population regime.

Time series data confirm that, in the low-mutation regime, the population is effectively characterized by a common strategy at each instant (Fig.~\ref{fig:evidence_of_common_strategy}). The apparent breadth of the long-term strategy distribution arises from averaging over time and across realizations, not from coexistence of widely different strategies at a single moment.

This quasi-monomorphic behavior justifies interpreting the evolutionary process as a stochastic trajectory in strategy space, driven by rare successful mutations and shaped by extinction events. The fact that extinction events typically occur while the population is near a single strategy further supports this coarse-grained description.

\section{Explanation of the evolutionary outcome and a heuristic approximation} \label{sec:explanation_and_heuristic_approx}

To understand the evolutionary outcome, we return to fixed-strategy simulations and examine how growth and extinction scale with population size. While the mean growth rate $\langle\mu\rangle$ depends only weakly on $N_i$, the extinction rate $r_\text{ext}$ decreases approximately as a power law, $r_\text{ext}\sim N_i^{-\alpha}$. This strong size dependence implies that extinction pressure is significant for small populations but rapidly weakens as $N_i$ increases.

This observation suggests that the evolutionary attractor emerges from the competition between exponential growth amplification and algebraically suppressed extinction risk. Motivated by this idea, we propose the heuristic stationary distribution
\begin{equation} \label{eq:heuristic_strategy_distribution}
    p(\overline{s})\propto\frac{\exp(N_\text{ini}\cdot\langle\mu(\overline{s})\rangle)}{r_\text{ext}(N_\text{ini},\overline{s})+p_\text{mut}\cdot\langle\mu_b(\overline{s})\rangle},
\end{equation}
constructed solely from quantities measured in fixed-strategy simulations.

The numerator captures the amplification of early growth differences when multiple strategies compete after reinitialization, while the denominator approximates the rate at which a strategy disappears, either through population extinction or mutation-driven replacement. Despite its simplicity, this expression reproduces the qualitative location and sharpening of the evolved distribution as $N_i$ increases.

\section{Discussion and outlook} \label{sec:discussion}

Our results provide a minimal and explicit example of how evolutionary outcomes in finite populations cannot be inferred solely from average instantaneous growth. The long-term dominant strategy depends not only on mean behavior, but also on the magnitude and direction of fluctuations, particularly those leading to extinction. In fluctuating environments with bounded population size, extinction is unavoidable over long times and therefore acts as a genuine selective force.

As population size increases, extinction risk becomes negligible and classical growth maximization is recovered. For smaller populations, however, extinction risk reshapes the adaptive landscape and biases evolution toward safer, lower-growth strategies. This crossover illustrates how demographic constraints fundamentally alter evolutionary optimization.

The heuristic approximation proposed here clarifies the competing scaling behaviors underlying this transition, even though it does not provide an exact quantitative description. The remaining discrepancies indicate that evolutionary dynamics cannot, in general, be reduced entirely to fixed-strategy observables. Developing a more systematic coarse-grained theory of strategy dynamics, possibly linked to quasi-stationary distributions, remains an important direction for future work.

More broadly, this study highlights that the effective fitness governing evolution in realistic finite populations must incorporate extinction risk explicitly. Understanding how such survival-weighted fitness functions emerge from stochastic ecological dynamics is essential for building a predictive theory of adaptation in fluctuating environments.
